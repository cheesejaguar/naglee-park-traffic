# Traffic safety in Naglee Park: four decades of data, interventions, and what actually works

**San Jose's Naglee Park neighborhood sits at a crossroads of urban traffic safety challenges — a historic residential grid bordered by a freeway, a major university, and arterial streets designed for speed rather than safety.** Despite being one of the most proactively traffic-calmed neighborhoods in the city (with 43 concrete medians installed in the 1980s and speed humps added throughout the 1990s), the broader downtown area has seen traffic fatalities climb **37% between 2008 and 2019** while population grew less than 10%. The city hit a modern record of **65 traffic deaths in 2022**, with pedestrians accounting for nearly half. A recent decline — 41 deaths in 2025, the lowest since 2012 — offers cautious optimism, driven by over **$90 million in safety investments** and more than 1,000 completed safety projects since 2021. Evidence from San Jose's own interventions and peer-reviewed research points toward a convergent set of solutions: lower speed limits, automated enforcement, physical street redesigns, and systematic neighborhood-scale deployment of traffic calming.

## Four decades of infrastructure changes reshaped the neighborhood

Naglee Park's traffic safety story begins in **1983**, when the neighborhood's Campus Community Association funded its own traffic study after the City of San Jose refused to do so. The study confirmed what residents already knew — cut-through traffic from San Jose State University and surrounding arterials was making residential streets dangerous. By the late 1980s, the city had approved and installed **43 concrete medians** throughout the neighborhood to narrow lanes and slow through-traffic. Volunteer "Median Buddies" planted and maintained these medians, creating the distinctive landscaped islands that still define the neighborhood. Through the 1990s, **speed humps** were added on multiple residential streets, forming a layered traffic-calming network unusual for San Jose at the time.

The opening of **I-280 through central San Jose in 1972** had already fundamentally shaped the neighborhood's traffic patterns, creating a hard southern boundary and funneling interchange traffic through 10th and 11th Streets. VTA light rail phased in between 1987 and 1991, altering transit access along the western approaches to the neighborhood. But the most transformative infrastructure changes came in the late 2010s and 2020s, driven by the city's Better Bikeways program and a massive pavement initiative.

**Better Bikeways Phase I (2018–2019)** installed approximately 10 miles of protected bike lanes throughout downtown, including on **San Fernando Street** (Naglee Park's northern boundary) and portions of **10th and 11th Streets** (the western boundary). These "quick-build" installations used paint and plastic bollards at a total cost of just $1.5 million. Phase II brought permanent upgrades: the **10th and 11th Street Bikeway** was completed in January 2023 with concrete curb-protected lanes, 14 transit boarding islands, and protected intersection treatments. Construction on the **San Fernando Better Bikeway** — upgrading plastic bollards to permanent concrete islands with protected intersections — began in March 2026.

The voter-approved **2016 Measure B** (county sales tax), **2018 Measure T** (city bond), and State **Senate Bill 1** unlocked unprecedented pavement funding. San Jose repaved nearly **300 miles of streets in 2019 alone** — more than in the previous two decades combined. The city's pavement condition index rose from 66 in 2018 to 73 by 2023, the highest ever recorded. A 9-year plan aims to maintain all 1,552 miles of local streets by 2028, meaning most Naglee Park streets have been or will be resurfaced.

Looking ahead, several major projects will further reshape the area. The **VTA Central Bikeway** will bring a 10.8-mile Class IV separated bikeway along 10th/11th Streets with approximately 50 raised crosswalks and 20 protected intersections. The **Willow-Keyes Complete Street Project** (fall 2026) will implement a road diet with roundabouts south of Naglee Park. The **Julian-Saint James couplet conversion** (2027–2028) will return one-way streets to two-way traffic north of downtown, with high-visibility crosswalks and accessible curb ramps.

San Jose's traffic crash picture is stark, even if recent trends show improvement. The city's official data paints a clear trajectory:

| 2023 | 49 | 27 |
| 2024 | 49 | — |

The **2022 record** was influenced by post-pandemic factors: reduced congestion enabling higher speeds, increased unhoused population exposure, and economic reopening effects. Pedestrians consistently account for **40–50% of all traffic fatalities**, a proportion San Jose Police describe as "historically high." The downtown area near SJSU ranks as the **second most dangerous zone for cyclists in Silicon Valley**, with 97 bicycle crashes between 2011 and 2021 producing 92 injuries and 1 death. The city averages roughly **20 crashes per day** across all types.

A troubling equity dimension compounds the data. Unhoused traffic fatalities **tripled from 5 in 2018 to 17 in 2021**, with 71% involving pedestrians and 82% occurring at night. Fourteen priority safety corridors — just 3% of the city's streets — account for **half of all fatal crashes**, and most pass through Equity Priority Communities. San Jose's intersection crash rate of **45.69%** runs 23% higher than the California city average, though individual intersection crashes tend to be less severe than average.

For Naglee Park specifically, granular neighborhood-level data must be extracted from the **San Jose Open Data Portal** (crash records since 1977, updated weekly) or UC Berkeley's **TIMS** mapping system by drawing a geographic polygon around the neighborhood boundaries. No pre-tabulated reports exist for this specific area. However, specific incidents near the neighborhood highlight ongoing risks: a pedestrian was killed at **Santa Clara Street and 10th Street** in April 2023 (a driver making a left turn struck someone in a marked crosswalk), and a pedestrian was critically injured at **South Third and East San Carlos** in August 2025.

## Speeding dominates crash causation, with design failures enabling it

The data on crash causation in San Jose points overwhelmingly to speed as the primary killer. **Speeding is the number-one known factor in fatal and severe-injury crashes**, appearing in **33% of fatalities between 2018 and 2022**. Unsafe turning ranks second, and red-light running third. An analysis of approximately 12,987 crash records from San Jose's open data reveals the full collision picture:

Rear-end collisions account for **25.8%** of all crashes, followed by sideswipes (20.3%), broadsides (20.1%), and hit-object crashes (8.9%). Vehicle-bicycle crashes make up 6.0% and vehicle-pedestrian crashes 5.6% — small percentages that belie their outsized severity. Hit-and-run incidents comprise a staggering **25% of all crashes**. Driver violation is the primary collision factor in **69% of cases**, and 90% of crashes are associated with human factors, errors, and poor choices.

Environmental conditions tell a counterintuitive story: **75% of crashes occur in clear weather** and 77% on dry roadways. More than half of traffic fatalities occur during evening and nighttime hours, with 29.5% of all crashes happening in darkness with street lighting present. This underscores that crash patterns are driven primarily by behavior and road design rather than weather conditions.

For pedestrian crashes specifically, SJPD reports that "in most cases the pedestrian was jaywalking or crossing mid-block rather than crossing at an intersection." But this framing obscures a critical design factor: controlled crossings on San Jose arterials occur on average only **every 2,000 feet**, forcing pedestrians to choose between long detours and dangerous mid-block crossings. The city's Vision Zero program manager has acknowledged that San Jose was "built out for cars" with wide, multi-lane arterials designed for high-speed throughput — the very streets that concentrate fatalities.

Within Naglee Park, resident reports on community forums document persistent speeding on streets like South 14th Street and cut-through traffic using residential streets to bypass congestion on surrounding arterials. The neighborhood's existing calming infrastructure (medians and speed humps) moderates but does not eliminate these patterns. Vision Zero Program Manager Jesse Mintz-Roth has specifically cited Naglee Park's calming islands as effective, while noting that the neighborhood's "political connections" helped it secure improvements that other neighborhoods lack.

## A layered system of programs targets pedestrian safety from multiple angles

San Jose's pedestrian safety infrastructure has evolved from a city with no coordinated strategy to one deploying a multi-layered system of programs, grants, and policy tools. The city adopted **Vision Zero in 2015** — the fourth U.S. city to do so — and unanimously approved an updated **2025 Vision Zero Action Plan** on February 25, 2025, targeting a **30% reduction in fatalities and severe injuries by 2030** and complete elimination by 2040. The plan adopts the federal **Safe System Approach** and identifies five priority action areas: equity and vulnerable road users, engineering for safety, community engagement, data-driven decision-making, and policy advocacy.

The **Walk Safe San José** pedestrian safety plan focuses specifically on City Council Districts 3, 5, 6, and 7 — the districts with the most traffic fatalities — with **District 3 encompassing downtown and the area adjacent to Naglee Park**. The program uses quick-build materials (paint, flex posts, signage) and has gathered 311 online survey responses and held four demonstration pop-ups. The city's **Walk n' Roll program** operates at more than 250 schools, including **Horace Mann Elementary** in Naglee Park, where **Viva EscuelaSJ events** periodically close surrounding streets to cars during morning drop-off so children can walk and bike safely.

Federal funding has been transformative. In December 2023, San Jose received a **$12.9 million Safe Streets and Roads for All (SS4A) grant** covering four project locations, including **East Santa Clara Street at 11th Street** — directly at Naglee Park's western boundary and one of the highest-injury intersections downtown. The project will install a pedestrian refuge island, sidewalk extension, bulb-out corner, signal modification, and raised bikeway, with construction anticipated in late 2027. A separate **$8.5 million SS4A grant** funds the city's speed camera program.

Perhaps the most consequential new tool is **automated speed enforcement under AB 645**, a state law authorizing San Jose as one of six California cities to pilot speed cameras through 2032. The city plans **33 cameras** in school zones and high-injury corridors, with income-adjusted fines and no DMV points. Cameras became operational in 2026. Combined with **AB 43** (enabling lower speed limits near schools and high-injury corridors) and **AB 413** (mandating daylighting — no parking within 20 feet of crosswalks — statewide), California's recent legislative wave gives San Jose tools that were unavailable just three years ago.

Community advocacy organizations have been critical drivers. The **Silicon Valley Bicycle Coalition** (founded 1993) organized a coalition letter demanding full Vision Zero funding and has conducted infrastructure "privilege rides" for newly elected council members. **California Walks / Walk San José** has performed walk audits and supported the Downtown Transportation Plan since 2012. The **Naglee Park Campus Community Association** itself has a long history of traffic safety engagement dating to the 1983 traffic study that launched the median program.

## Before-and-after evidence shows what works in San Jose

San Jose now has its own growing evidence base for traffic safety interventions, complementing the extensive national research literature. The most documented local success is the **Senter Road quick-build project (2020–2021)**, where painted intersections, delineator posts, and plastic barriers reduced killed-or-seriously-injured (KSI) crashes from **15 in 2020 to 5 in 2021 — a 67% reduction** — at a cost under $1 million. The city has since secured a $10 million state earmark to make these improvements permanent with concrete separators, median islands, enhanced crosswalks, and upgraded lighting.

The **Lincoln Avenue road diet** in Willow Glen (2015) received rigorous academic evaluation from San Jose State University's Mineta Transportation Institute. The 4-to-3 lane conversion produced falling traffic volumes and reduced speeding within the treatment zone, with "limited overall negative impacts" on surrounding streets, though some individual side streets saw increased traffic. The **Fruitdale Avenue lane reduction** in District 6 eliminated serious incidents after a stretch that had seen several severe injuries and at least one death between 2016 and 2019. On **Branham Lane**, one of the city's deadliest corridors, plastic barriers that narrowed lanes reduced drag racing and fatalities, with Councilmember Pam Foley reporting: "We used to see drag racing, but the streets narrowed so it's not as attractive to speed."

National research provides strong quantitative support for these local observations. The **FHWA designates road diets as a Proven Safety Countermeasure** with documented crash reductions of **19–47%**. A **Cochrane systematic review of 35 speed camera studies** found injury crash reductions of **20–25%** at fixed sites and **30–40% reductions in fatal/serious injury crashes** across wider areas. The speed-fatality relationship is exponential: a pedestrian struck at 20 mph faces a **5% chance of death**; at 30 mph, that rises to **45%**; at 40 mph, **90%**. A single 1-mph reduction in average speed correlates with a **5–6% reduction in crashes**.

For 20 mph speed limits specifically — a policy directly applicable to Naglee Park's residential streets — a 2025 meta-analysis found they reduced traffic crashes by an average of **26.5%** overall. London's implementation across 399 zones showed a **42% reduction in all casualties**. Even sign-only schemes (without physical changes) reduced mean speed by 1.6 mph and casualties by nearly 11%, though physical traffic calming dramatically amplifies effectiveness.

## Evidence converges on a clear hierarchy of solutions for neighborhoods like Naglee Park

The research literature, FHWA proven countermeasures, NACTO design guidelines, and San Jose's own experience converge on a clear set of evidence-based recommendations for residential neighborhoods with Naglee Park's characteristics — a regular grid, proximity to a university and freeway, mix of local and cut-through traffic, and existing but incomplete traffic calming.

**The highest-impact interventions combine speed reduction with physical infrastructure changes.** NACTO recommends **20 mph target speeds** for residential streets and emphasizes that speed management must be self-enforcing through design rather than relying solely on posted limits. The optimal approach layers multiple treatments systematically across the entire neighborhood rather than on individual streets, preventing traffic displacement.

The most applicable and evidence-backed solutions, ranked by impact and feasibility, include area-wide 20 mph speed limits (enabled by AB 43, producing **21–42% crash reductions** at very low cost), automated speed enforcement on boundary arterials (producing **20–54% crash reductions** under the AB 645 pilot), high-visibility crosswalk markings at every grid intersection (**37% crash reduction** per San Francisco's empirical Bayes study), raised crosswalks and speed tables at key intersections (producing **5–14 mph speed reductions**), and compliance with California's new daylighting mandate with physical curb extensions (associated with **41% pedestrian injury reductions** in New York City data).

The neighborhood's grid pattern makes it an ideal candidate for **neighborhood greenways** — low-volume streets systematically enhanced with traffic calming, wayfinding, shared-lane markings, and traffic diverters. Portland's experience shows traffic diverters alone can reduce cut-through volumes by **69%**. Streets like 13th, 14th, or 16th could serve as north-south greenways connecting SJSU to parks and community destinations. Naglee Park's existing **street tree canopy** provides passive speed reduction — a German study of landscaped center strips with narrower lanes found **30% fewer overall accidents** and **60% fewer injury accidents**.

Protected bike lanes on boundary streets (San Fernando, 10th/11th) — already being built through Better Bikeways — address cyclist safety, with a University of British Columbia study finding they reduce injury risk by up to **90%** compared to streets with no cycling infrastructure. Leading pedestrian intervals at signalized boundary intersections offer a **13% pedestrian crash reduction** at near-zero cost (signal timing change only). Pedestrian refuge islands on wider streets provide one of the strongest documented safety effects in the FHWA's crash modification factor database.

## Conclusion: Naglee Park's path forward builds on proven foundations

Naglee Park occupies an unusual position in San Jose's traffic safety landscape. Its 1983-era medians and 1990s speed humps made it a pioneer in neighborhood traffic calming, and these investments continue to moderate speeds on interior streets. But the neighborhood's boundary streets — where most severe crashes concentrate — have only recently begun receiving the infrastructure upgrades that evidence shows are most effective. The completion of permanent protected bikeways on 10th, 11th, and San Fernando Streets, the planned SS4A improvements at Santa Clara and 11th, and the forthcoming speed camera program collectively represent the most significant safety investment in the area's history.

Three structural insights emerge from this research. First, **speed is the dominant variable** — it is the top known factor in San Jose's fatal crashes, and the exponential relationship between speed and pedestrian death risk means that even small speed reductions save lives. Second, **quick-build projects work**: San Jose's own Senter Road data showing 67% KSI reductions at under $1 million demonstrates that cities need not wait for expensive permanent construction. Third, **California's recent legislative changes** — AB 43, AB 645, and AB 413 — provide San Jose with tools that close gaps in the previous safety framework, particularly automated enforcement and lower speed limits that were previously legally constrained.

The gap between what evidence supports and what has been implemented remains significant. Area-wide 20 mph speed limits, systematic raised crosswalks across the residential grid, neighborhood greenway designations, and full deployment of speed cameras on high-injury boundary streets would collectively address the leading causes of crashes in and around Naglee Park. The data, the research, and the legal authority now exist. What remains is sustained political will and funding to match the scale of the problem.
